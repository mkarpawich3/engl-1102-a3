Oroonoko's Suicide

Published on April 14th at 9:24 PM by Aphra Behn

Oroonoko died on Tuesday.


I... do not even know what I should write. How could I? Oroonoko is dead. Tuesday was the death of a king. A king of human nature, the epitome of what we could be. I was not there to witness it, but I heard from others: they said Oroonoko died from his own hand. I can never know for sure, but I shall nonetheless share what I know with you, my reader, so that you too can remember when such a man once walked this Earth.

Towards the afternoon, several reported that Oroonoko had gone missing. Disappeared. A search party was organized immediately, made up of those who he could call friend. One said they had seen him escape into the forest, gripping a knife in his right hand so that the knuckle turned white. And there they found him, lying on the ground in a pool of his own blood, knife in hand. But he had not died just yet. Nature would not give such relief to him.

Soon after the search party had discovered Oroonoko, Governor Briam and a few other men entered, wielding their own weapons. And they shot him. I am told they shot him good, for every man had run out of ammunition by the time the massacre was over. I can hardly tell the next part. But I must, so that the others may hear his story. I am told they cut up his body... carried each limb off and disappeared. I am told they had grins on their faces, laughing like the devil as they severed limb from torso, head from neck.

Make no mistake though: Oroonoko had destined for himself to die. Had none found him there in the thicket, time would have been his executioner. It matters not who pulled the trigger. For Oroonoko had compelled them to do it, begged them to. Only Imoinda mattered to him in those final moments. Only her. His spirit may have left this Earth, but we know that it has gone where Oroonoko wished himself to be. And that is all we can really ask for, is it not?

I am afraid that our journey has at last come to an end, my dear reader. I hope that you are where you wish to be in your life, as our beloved Oroonoko is now.

Yours,

A. Behn
